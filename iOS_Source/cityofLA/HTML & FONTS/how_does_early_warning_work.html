With the ShakeAlert system, there are hundreds of seismometers installed throughout California, Oregon, and Washington along the many faults that can produce earthquakes. When shaking begins, the location and magnitude of an earthquake is rapidly estimated by analyzing the first shaking measured by sensors near the source. All earthquakes start small and initially grow at the same rate. It is not possible to determine immediately after an earthquake starts how large it will eventually become. Once an earthquake is detected, the expected ground- shaking severity across a region is estimated, and alerts are sent to local populations before larger, more damaging shaking arrives. The ShakeAlert System continues to refine these ground motion estimates and updates its alert as the earthquake grows and more sensor data become available. Typically, more distant locations will have more time to act but will experience less intense shaking. The USGS has been developing the ShakeAlert System for the West Coast since 2006, jointly with partners from state governments, universities, and private foundations. ShakeAlert centers in Seattle, the San Francisco Bay area and the Los Angeles area, but are not fully integrated across the West Coast, but the system is not yet issuing public alerts.

The ShakeAlert system provides real time earthquake alerting. Because the system is in real time, occasionally it can behave in ways that we may not expect it to. You might feel shaking but did not get an alert or you might get an alert and not feel shaking.

Types of alerts you may see:

  • Correct alert: Correct level of shaking sent to trigger alert
  • Late warning zone: Geographical area that may too close to the epicenter of the earthquake to get an alert
  • False alert: Sufficiently inaccurate shaking information (not a seismic event, over- or under-estimate of ground shaking, teleseismic event, or event splitting)
  • Late alert: Alert arrives after shaking is felt (speed of delivery mechanism is slow or too close to epicenter)
  • Missed alert: No alert with felt shaking (shaking under-estimated or threshold is too high)
  • Cancelled alert: Ground motion determined to be non-seismic or technical error (quarry blast or ground noise)
Magnitude (Mag) represents the amount of energy released from the earthquake and depends of the surface area of the rupture. Seismic intensity is the amount of shaking (MMI), is relevant measure of potential damage which is dependent on your location to the epicenter, depth of earthquake and soil effects. When an earthquake reaches the pre-set threshold of Mag and/or Intensity it triggers an alert.
