The ShakeAlertâ„¢ Earthquake Early Warning System (EEW) is under development by United States Geological Survey (USGS) and its academic partners at the California Institute of Technology; the University of California, Berkeley; the University of Oregon; and the University of Washington. This development includes in close partnership and collaboration with State Partners from California, Oregon, and Washington Emergency Management and Geological Surveys.

The USGS, in collaboration, with its academic partners run the seismic network which is a system of sensors that detects an earthquake when it starts. When an earthquake starts it produces two kinds of seismic waves that carry energy through the Earth. The waves are detected by the sensors which send the information to a computer system faster than the seismic waves travel. The system analyzes the ground motion data and provides an earthquake alert which includes rapid estimates of the magnitude, location, and expected intensity of shaking. Earthquake magnitude (Mag) represents the amount of energy released and depends on the surface area of the rupture. Seismic intensity (MMI) is the amount of shaking, and is a relevant measure for potential damage. When the earthquake reaches the pre-set threshold of magnitude and intensity of shaking an alert is triggered. The amount of warning time depends on the speed of the warning mechanism and your distance from the earthquake. You may receive as little as a few seconds to minutes of warning time.
